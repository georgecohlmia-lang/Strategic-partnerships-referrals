Your Partnership and Referral Plan

Relationships, not deals. Recommendation, not infrastructure. The product is the program.

01 Name your peer founders

Three to five people whose work you genuinely read and whose audience overlaps yours. Not aspirational names — real peers. Be honest about the relationship state.

Your peer network

Other founders, writers, operators at your stage whose audience overlaps yours. The finding method: list the people whose new piece you actually open when it lands — not aspirational names — then narrow to those at your stage whose audience overlaps yours. The cultivation method: become a genuine reader first — reference their work where it fits, comment with something that adds, send the occasional note. The relationship-state dropdown is the honesty mechanism — if you can't articulate audience overlap concretely, the peer doesn't belong here.

0 peers — aim for 3 to 5.
ExampleA founder writing about regenerative agriculture — adjacent category, audience reads about food systems · A condiment subscription brand founder — adjacent DTC business model, audience cares about supply chain
02 Identify your recommender relationships

Three to seven people whose recommendation would carry disproportionate weight in your category. Some may already actively recommend you. Most are probably aspirational — that's fine, and honest absence is the assignment too.

Your recommenders

Editors of respected publications. Established founders whose audience trusts their judgment. Writers with substantially larger reach. Asymmetric by design — their recommendation matters far more to you than yours to them. The method, since you cannot ask: identify the 3–7 whose specific audience is the one you want (not the most famous), engage with their work seriously over months, and produce at least one piece so good it's genuinely worth recommending — usually the anchor piece. Then wait, visibly doing the work.

0 recommenders — aim for 3 to 7.
ExampleEditor of an independent food-business publication — actively recommends · Category-veteran founder 10 years ahead — knows my work but hasn't recommended yet · Substack writer with much larger audience — aspirational, working toward it
The realistic assessment

The work that produces recommender relationships is the work whose return you cannot see for a long time. Be specific about where your work is today versus where it needs to be.

Be honest. If your anchor pieces aren't yet at the depth a respected editor would link to, name that. If your category perspective isn't yet distinctive enough, name that. The named gap is what guides the six-month production plan.
ExampleMy anchor pieces are getting deeper, but I've only published two at the four-thousand-word depth that the editor would naturally link. I need three or four more at that depth in the next six months, on questions only I can answer from inside the grocery-chain experience, before I'd expect his recommendation to repeat reliably.
03 Sketch your customer referral motion

One line. A personal-ask discipline. And the formal infrastructure you're explicitly choosing NOT to build. Programs do not fix products that aren't referrable; the product is the program.

The one line

A single hand-written line in your post-purchase communication (or its equivalent moment). No incentive, no code, no tracking. Just an invitation.

If you don't have a post-purchase email specifically, name the equivalent moment — the natural point of communication right after a customer has experienced the value, when asking for a referral feels organic.
Example"If you like what you receive, the best compliment is forwarding this to a friend who would too — here is the page to send them."
The personal-ask discipline

Not a list of customer names. A discipline. What kind of customer will you personally ask, and at what moment? The moment matters more than the list.

The lesson's framing: personally, in the right moment, no incentive. Name the qualifying signal that tells you a customer is ready to be asked — and the natural moment to ask.
ExampleCustomers who have been subscribed for 6+ months and have replied to two or more of my emails. Moment: when they email me with a specific positive observation about a recent box, I'll thank them and ask if there's anyone they'd recommend it to.
What you're explicitly NOT building

The trap is partnership infrastructure that looks like motion and produces nothing. Check each form of formal infrastructure you are explicitly committing to not build.

The reasoning anchors the commitment. Naming the why makes it easier to hold the discipline when the corporate-reflex pull arrives — which it will.
ExampleNone of these would earn their place at my scale — the operational cost would exceed any return, and they would signal to my actual recommenders that I've replaced relationship work with transactional infrastructure. I'll revisit in two or three years if the relationships have produced enough to justify formalizing.
